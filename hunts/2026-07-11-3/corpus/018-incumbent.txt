David Ryder/Getty images Microsoft's commercial price increases for Microsoft 365 are in effect as of today, July 1, 2026 &mdash; the largest pricing update to the platform since 2022. For organizations renewing after today, new per-user rates apply immediately. The lock-in window for preserving old pricing closed yesterday at midnight. Here is the complete breakdown of what changed, who it hits hardest, and what every IT and finance team should still be doing before their next renewal date. New prices apply to any new subscription or renewal signed on or after July 1, 2026. Existing customers on active annual or multi-year agreements keep current rates until their next renewal date. The full breakdown for the most widely used commercial plans, in USD per user per month on an annual commitment: Plan Old Price New Price Increase Microsoft 365 Business Basic $6.00 $7.00 +17% Microsoft 365 Business Standard $12.50 $14.00 +12% Microsoft 365 Business Premium $22.00 $22.00 No change Office 365 E1 $10.00 $10.00 No change Office 365 E3 $23.00 $26.00 +13% Microsoft 365 E3 $36.00 $39.00 +8% Microsoft 365 E5 $57.00 $60.00 +5% Microsoft 365 F1 (with Teams) $2.25 $3.00 +33% Microsoft 365 F1 (without Teams) $1.75 $2.50 +43% Microsoft 365 F3 (with Teams) $8.00 $10.00 +25% All prices are per user per month, annual commitment, USD list price. Standalone add-ons are also moving: Windows Enterprise per device jumps 31% (from $5.85 to $7.63), and Microsoft 365 Apps for Business rises 21% (from $8.25 to $10.00). Standalone Microsoft Teams and Copilot SKUs are excluded from this update &mdash; those prices hold for now. Frontline Workers Face the Biggest Hit by Percentage The F1 plan without Teams carries the steepest increase at 43%, moving from $1.75 to $2.50 per user per month. That sounds modest in isolation, but at scale it is not. A hospital, retailer, or manufacturer with 5,000 frontline workers on F1 is looking at an additional $45,000 per year on that single SKU alone. Organizations with large shift-worker or field-worker populations should audit Teams usage before their next renewal. If those workers are not actively using Teams, the with-Teams variant at $3.00 still carries a 33% increase but provides a more defensible long-term choice if Teams adoption picks up. Office 365 E3 carries the steepest enterprise percentage increase at 13%, moving from $23 to $26. For a 500-seat organization, that difference alone adds roughly $18,000 per year at the base rate. What Microsoft Is Bundling to Justify the Increases Nicole Herskowitz , Microsoft's Corporate Vice President for Microsoft 365 and Copilot, framed the update as a value expansion rather than a pure cost hike when announcing the changes last December. New capabilities are rolling into existing tiers, with full deployment complete by August 1, 2026. E3 customers gain Microsoft Defender for Office 365 Plan 1 &mdash; the anti-phishing, Safe Links, and malware protection layer that previously ran around $2 per user per month as a standalone add-on &mdash; plus Intune Remote Help, Intune Advanced Analytics, and Intune Plan 2 capabilities. Organizations already paying separately for Defender for Office 365 Plan 1 will see some direct offset at their next renewal. E5 customers receive Security Copilot at 400 Security Compute Units per 1,000 licensed users per month. Security Compute Units are metered AI processing capacity, not seat counts; intensive investigation workflows and automated agent sessions consume them faster than standard use. Microsoft charges $6 per additional SCU beyond the included allocation. E5 customers should model Security Copilot consumption before renewals to avoid unexpected pay-as-you-go charges. Business Basic and Standard customers gain 50 GB of additional mailbox storage, URL time-of-click protection in Outlook, and Copilot Chat enhancements that include inbox and calendar awareness plus access to Word, Excel, and PowerPoint agents. A detail that matters: Microsoft confirms that all qualifying SKUs receive the new features as they roll out regardless of the price paid or agreement term. Organizations that locked in old pricing before July 1 lose no features and pay less. For those who did not renew early, the price increase and new features arrive together at the next renewal. Microsoft removed Enterprise Agreement volume discounts in November 2025 &mdash; a separate change that compounded on top of the December pricing announcement without being included in the headline figures. Under the old EA structure, organizations at Level D pricing (15,000 or more seats) received automatic 12% discounts on online services. Level C customers received roughly 9%, Level B roughly 6%. As of November 1, 2025, all EA customers pay the same Level A list price, regardless of size. According to SAMexpert , a Microsoft licensing consultancy, a 24,000-user E5 organization that previously held Level D pricing could face roughly $2 million more per year from the discount removal alone &mdash; before factoring in the July price increase now in effect. The combined impact for large enterprises pushes the effective rate of change into the range of 20%, well above the 5-8% headline figures for E3 and E5. Organizations that locked in multi-year Enterprise Agreements before November 2025 are shielded through their current term. Everyone else is negotiating at list price. What Month-to-Month Subscribers Should Know Month-to-month subscribers will see the new rates at their next billing cycle after July 1. For most, that means the August invoice. Annual subscribers with renewal dates after today keep current pricing through their renewal date. Consumer Microsoft 365 plans &mdash; Personal at $69.99 per year and Family at $99.99 per year &mdash; are not part of this update. Education pricing is also unchanged. Nonprofit pricing adjusts proportionally with commercial rates through a fixed percentage discount structure. Government plans face increases, but Microsoft has confirmed that for suites where the increase exceeds 10%, the adjustment is phased in over multiple years in line with federal regulations. What You Can Still Do Before Your Next Renewal The early-renewal window closed yesterday, but that is not the end of the options available to renewing organizations. Audit seat count before you renew. Most organizations carry unused licenses &mdash; departed employees, role changes, applications that never got adopted. A 90-day sign-in activity report from the Microsoft 365 Admin Center surfaces unused seats quickly. Trimming those before renewal reduces the base against which the new price multiplies. Revisit your plan tier. The narrowing gap between Business Standard ($14) and Business Premium ($22) &mdash; now just $8 &mdash; makes Premium worth fresh consideration for organizations with remote workforces or any device management burden. Run the comparison with current numbers, not prior-year assumptions. Check add-ons for overlap. E3 customers paying separately for Defender for Office 365 Plan 1 should drop the standalone add-on at their next renewal &mdash; it is being folded into the base plan by August 1. This will not eliminate the price increase, but it partially offsets it. For large Frontline deployments, model with-Teams vs. without-Teams. The 43% increase on F1 without Teams against a 33% increase for the Teams variant tightens the economics considerably for any workforce actively using Teams. Request a usage analytics export now. Organizations with renewal dates in the second half of 2026 have time to build a data-backed case for their license mix before entering renewal negotiations. Starting that work today &mdash; rather than when the invoice arrives &mdash; is the only leverage remaining. Frequently Asked Questions When do Microsoft 365 price increases take effect? New prices apply to all new subscriptions and renewals signed on or after July 1, 2026. Existing customers on active annual or multi-year agreements keep current pricing until their next renewal date. Month-to-month subscribers will see the increase reflected on their August invoice. Which Microsoft 365 business plans are affected by this pricing update? The increase covers most commercial Business, Enterprise, and Frontline suites, including Microsoft 365 Business Basic, Business Standard, Office 365 E3, Microsoft 365 E3 and E5, and the F1 and F3 Frontline plans. Microsoft 365 Business Premium and Office 365 E1 are holding flat. Consumer, education, and Microsoft 365 Copilot standalone pricing are not changing. How does Microsoft 365 pricing now compare to Google Workspace? Google raised its own prices in early 2025 when it bundled Gemini AI into all plans. Current Google Workspace Business Starter is $7 per user per month on an annual commitment; Business Standard is $14 per user per month. Microsoft 365 Business Basic is now also $7 per user per month and Business Standard is $14 &mdash; the plans are essentially price-matched at the entry tiers today. With the cost gap as narrow as it has been in years, migration costs and integration depth are the primary decision factors rather than base price. Is Microsoft 365 Copilot pricing changing? No. The standalone Copilot add-on at $30 per user per month is explicitly excluded from this pricing update.
