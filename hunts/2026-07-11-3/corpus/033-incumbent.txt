Increase Range 5 – 33% Across SaaS and enterprise Key Driver AI Tax AI features bundled into pricing Scale and Scope of Price Increases The 2025-2026 cycle marks a continuation and intensification of the pricing trends that began in 2024. Microsoft alone is implementing increases across its entire M365 portfolio effective July 2026, with frontline worker licenses seeing jumps of 25-33% and business tiers rising 12-17%. Combined with the November 2025 removal of Enterprise Agreement volume discounts (worth up to 12%), large organizations face a double hit on their Microsoft spend. VMware&#8217;s transformation under Broadcom remains the single most disruptive force, with customers reporting net increases of 150% to 1,200% as perpetual licenses are forcibly converted to subscription bundles. The new 72-core minimum for vSphere Standard (up from 16) disproportionately impacts SMBs, while a 20% late renewal penalty adds further financial pressure. Beyond these headline-grabbers, the breadth of increases is striking. Google Workspace raised prices 17-22% in early 2025. Atlassian is implementing 5-15% cloud increases with Data Center hikes reaching 15-40%. Adobe restructured Creative Cloud with effective increases up to 27%. SAP, Oracle, IBM, Salesforce, ServiceNow, AWS, and Cisco all implemented their own rounds of increases, creating a compounding effect that no IT budget can absorb without strategic intervention. Adobe&#8217;s Creative Cloud Pro restructuring, ServiceNow&#8217;s Now Assist add-on (30-45%), and Microsoft&#8217;s Copilot ecosystem all follow this pattern. Whether customers use these AI features or not, they are paying for them. Subscription Enforcement: VMware&#8217;s forced migration from perpetual to subscription licensing continues to be the most aggressive example, but the pattern is industry-wide. Vendors are eliminating perpetual options and locking customers into recurring revenue streams with multi-year commitments. Discount Erosion: Microsoft&#8217;s removal of automatic EA volume discounts (November 2025) signals a broader trend where vendors are clawing back negotiated benefits. Large enterprises that relied on volume-based pricing are particularly exposed. Bundle Restructuring: Both VMware and Adobe are consolidating product lines into larger bundles, forcing customers to pay for capabilities they may not need. This approach inflates per-user costs while vendors claim &#8220;more value.&#8221; Impact by Category Virtualization: Remains the highest absolute impact category due to VMware&#8217;s ongoing restructuring under Broadcom. Organizations with significant VMware footprints face existential cost decisions. Productivity Suites: Both Microsoft and Google implemented broad increases across their entire productivity portfolios, affecting virtually every organization. The combined effect of M365 price hikes plus EA discount removal creates a compounding cost challenge. Creative &#038; Design: Adobe&#8217;s restructuring of Creative Cloud into Pro and Standard tiers represents a significant shift, with effective increases of up to 27% for users who need full AI capabilities. ERP &#038; Business Systems: SAP and Oracle continue their steady drumbeat of support fee increases, while SAP&#8217;s cloud ERP renewals carry 10%+ premiums. These increases are particularly painful because switching costs are prohibitively high. ITSM &#038; DevOps: ServiceNow&#8217;s annual uplift model (5-10%) combined with aggressive AI add-on pricing (30-45%) creates a dual cost pressure. Atlassian&#8217;s Data Center increases of 15-40% push organizations toward their cloud offerings. Major Vendor Analysis Microsoft Microsoft has executed a multi-layered pricing strategy for 2025-2026 that hits organizations from multiple angles. The headline is the July 2026 M365 price increase affecting virtually every SKU, from Business Basic (+16.7%) to Frontline F1 (+33%). But the more strategically significant move was the November 2025 removal of automatic volume-based discounts from Enterprise Agreements, which can add up to 12% to costs for large organizations at renewal. Combined with the Copilot add-on at $30/user/month and standalone product increases of 15-21%, Microsoft is systematically extracting more revenue per seat while positioning AI as the justification. VMware (Broadcom) VMware under Broadcom continues to be the most disruptive force in enterprise software pricing. The April 2025 mandate requiring all new deployments to use subscription-only licensing, combined with the consolidation from 8,000+ SKUs to a handful of bundles (VCF and VVF), has created reported price increases of 150% to 1,200%. The increase of minimum core requirements from 16 to 72 for vSphere Standard disproportionately impacts SMBs. A 20% late renewal penalty and pressure toward 3-5 year commitments further reduce customer flexibility. While Broadcom announced a 50% reduction in VCF per-core pricing (from $700 to $350), the bundled structure often results in net increases for customers who previously purchased standalone products. Oracle Oracle&#8217;s most significant pricing move is the ongoing enforcement of its per-employee Java licensing model, which requires organizations to license every employee regardless of whether they directly use Java. For a mid-sized company with 500 employees, this can mean annual Java licensing costs of approximately $90,000. Gartner predicts that by 2026, 20% of organizations using Java will face Oracle audits. Additionally, Oracle support renewal costs continue to climb at 4-15% annually, with cloud contracts offering fewer contractual protections against price resets than traditional on-premise agreements. IBM and Red Hat IBM has announced a global price harmonization effective January 2026, implementing approximately 6% increases across most of its software, hardware, and services portfolio. Notable exceptions include IBM i software maintenance (SWMA) at 10% and Cloud Security &#038; Compliance Center, which saw a dramatic 118% increase (from $8.24 to $18.00 per instance/month) in January 2025. Red Hat, as an IBM subsidiary, implemented a 10% increase on all software subscriptions for EUR and GBP customers in April 2025, attributed to foreign exchange fluctuations. SaaS Vendors (Salesforce, Adobe, ServiceNow, Atlassian) The SaaS sector shows a consistent pattern of annual price escalation. Salesforce implemented a measured 6% increase on Enterprise and Unlimited editions. Adobe restructured Creative Cloud into Pro and Standard tiers with effective increases of up to 27%, bundling generative AI features as the value justification. ServiceNow&#8217;s mandatory 5-10% annual uplift, combined with the Now Assist AI add-on at 30-45% premium, creates significant compounding costs. Atlassian&#8217;s approach is particularly aggressive on Data Center products (15-40% increases in February 2026), while cloud increases remain more moderate at 5-10%, clearly incentivizing cloud migration. Licensing Model Evolution The 2025-2026 cycle accelerates several fundamental shifts in how software is licensed and priced. The most visible is the continued elimination of perpetual licensing, with VMware&#8217;s forced subscription migration serving as the template that other vendors are watching closely. Organizations that built their IT infrastructure on the assumption of perpetual license ownership are facing a structural cost increase that goes far beyond typical annual adjustments. AI-driven pricing tiers represent the newest evolution. Adobe&#8217;s split of Creative Cloud into &#8220;Pro&#8221; and &#8220;Standard&#8221; tiers, ServiceNow&#8217;s consumption-based Now Assist pricing, and Microsoft&#8217;s $30/user Copilot add-on all establish a pattern where AI capabilities command premium pricing. This creates a two-tier market where organizations must decide whether AI features justify 20-45% cost premiums, often without clear ROI data. Oracle&#8217;s per-employee Java licensing model represents perhaps the most radical licensing model change, decoupling license costs from actual software usage entirely. This approach, if adopted by other vendors, could fundamentally alter how organizations calculate software costs, shifting from usage-based to headcount-based models that are far more difficult to optimize. The erosion of volume discounts, exemplified by Microsoft&#8217;s EA changes, signals that vendors are moving away from rewarding scale. Combined with minimum purchase requirements (VMware&#8217;s 72-core minimum), these changes disproportionately impact organizations at both ends of the size spectrum: SMBs that are forced to over-purchase, and large enterprises that lose their negotiated advantages. Strategic Recommendations Immediate Actions (0-3 Months) Conduct a comprehensive software asset inventory to understand your exact exposure to each vendor&#8217;s price increases. Review all contracts approaching renewal, particularly Microsoft EAs, VMware agreements, and Oracle support contracts. Assess Java usage across the organization to prepare for Oracle&#8217;s per-employee licensing enforcement. Lock in current pricing where possible through early renewals or multi-year commitments on favorable terms. Medium-Term Optimization (3-12 Months) Invest in software asset management (SAM) capabilities to gain continuous visibility into license consumption and compliance posture. Evaluate VMware alternatives including Proxmox, Nutanix, and Hyper-V for organizations facing the most severe Broadcom-related increases. Assess open-source Java distributions (Eclipse Temurin, Amazon Corretto, Azul Zulu) to reduce Oracle Java exposure. Negotiate AI feature opt-outs where possible, particularly for ServiceNow Now Assist and Adobe Creative Cloud Standard tiers. Long-Term Strategic Positioning (12+ Months) Develop a vendor diversification strategy that reduces concentration risk with any single vendor. Accelerate cloud-native architecture adoption to increase portability and reduce lock-in. Build internal capabilities around open-source alternatives for categories with the highest price pressure. Establish a dedicated software procurement function that treats vendor management as a strategic discipline rather than a tactical purchasing activity. Risk Management Priorities The 2025-2026 pricing landscape creates multiple overlapping risk categories that require systematic management. Vendor concentration risk is the most acute, as organizations heavily dependent on VMware, Microsoft, or Oracle face disproportionate exposure to pricing changes and licensing model shifts. The compounding effect of multiple vendor increases hitting simultaneously can overwhelm IT budgets that were planned based on historical 3-5% annual escalation assumptions. Compliance and audit risk has intensified, particularly with Oracle&#8217;s aggressive Java licensing enforcement and the prediction that 20% of Java-using organizations will face audits by 2026. VMware&#8217;s new licensing structure, with its minimum core requirements and bundle mandates, creates new compliance complexities that organizations must actively manage. Effective risk mitigation requires dependency assessment and mapping across the entire software portfolio, diversification planning for high-risk vendor categories, automated compliance monitoring to avoid audit penalties, and scenario-based budget planning that accounts for the full range of potential price increases. Organizations must prepare for continued pricing pressure and develop capabilities to respond proactively rather than reactively to vendor changes. Future Outlook The fundamental drivers behind the 2025-2026 price increases will persist and likely intensify. AI monetization is still in its early stages, and vendors will continue to bundle AI capabilities into existing products at premium prices. Market consolidation (Broadcom/VMware, Cisco/Splunk) reduces competitive pressure and gives acquirers pricing power. The shift from perpetual to subscription licensing is approaching completion for most major vendors, locking in recurring revenue streams that are structurally higher than the models they replace. Regulatory responses remain slow and limited in scope. The EU&#8217;s Digital Markets Act and similar regulations may eventually constrain some pricing practices, but relief for immediate cost pressures is unlikely. However, competitive responses from alternative vendors and open-source projects are accelerating as the cost differential with incumbent vendors widens. Organizations willing to invest in vendor diversification and architectural flexibility will find increasingly viable alternatives. The software vendor price increases of 2025-2026 represent a structural shift in enterprise software economics, not a temporary market adjustment. Success in this environment demands sophisticated software asset management capabilities, strategic vendor relationship management, and architectural flexibility that reduces switching costs and vendor dependencies. Organizations that respond strategically and proactively will achieve competitive advantages that extend beyond cost savings to include improved operational flexibility and enhanced strategic positioning in an increasingly expensive software environment.
