Microsoft 365 commercial prices rise on July 1, 2026. Microsoft announced a global pricing and packaging update for several of its business suites. Business Basic goes from $6.00 to $7.00 per user, a 16 percent increase . Business Standard goes from $12.50 to $14.00, a 12 percent increase . Apps for Business, the standalone Office app plan, jumps from $8.25 to $10.00, a 21 percent increase . There is one piece of good news on timing. Existing customers stay on their current pricing until their next renewal . The new rate does not hit your card on July 1. It hits when your term renews after that date. So the renewal is the moment that matters, and it is also your moment to act. A few dollars per seat may not sound alarming. On a tidy account, it is not. The problem is that almost no account is tidy, and the increase lands on every seat you are paying for, used or not. That is why the size of the hike is the wrong thing to worry about. It is not just Microsoft. Software costs are climbing everywhere. This is not a one-off from a single vendor. Software pricing is moving up across the board. Gartner forecasts that worldwide software spending will grow 15.1 percent in 2026, to about $1.44 trillion . The same forecast puts total worldwide IT spending up 13.5 percent, reaching $6.31 trillion . Read that as a signal, not a headline. Vendors have pricing power right now, and they are using it. Microsoft will not be the last renewal letter you get this year. So the skill that protects your budget is not haggling over one increase. It is keeping every subscription right-sized so each bump lands on a lean account, not a bloated one. Infographic showing worldwide software spending forecast to grow 15 percent in 2026 Why the price hike is not your real cost Your real cost is not the price per seat. It is the seats you pay for and nobody uses. This is the part most owners miss, because the renewal letter only shows the new rate. It never shows the dozen logins that have not been touched in months. The data here is blunt. Zylo&#x27;s 2024 SaaS Management Index found that companies use only about half of the software licenses they buy . Its 2025 index found that organizations waste an average of $21 million a year on unused SaaS licenses, up 14.2 percent year over year . License waste is now the single biggest software-spend challenge IT teams name, with about seven in ten making it their top priority . Those headline numbers are enterprise-scale, so translate them down. The waste is a ratio, not a big-company problem: if half of what you buy goes unused, a small firm feels that just as hard per dollar. Even the smallest companies in Zylo&#x27;s data lost an average of $2 million on unused licenses . Now apply a 12 to 21 percent increase on top of that idle half. You are not just wasting money. You are about to waste more of it. Infographic showing companies use only about half of the software licenses they buy Where the waste hides in your Microsoft 365 tenant Most Microsoft 365 waste hides in four simple places. None of them are exotic. They are the everyday drift of a growing business, and they are easy to find once you look. Seats for people who left. A license assigned to a departed employee keeps billing until someone removes it. These orphaned seats are the most common form of waste. Over-tiered users. Someone on Business Standard who only needs email and Teams could sit on Business Basic. You are paying for apps they never open. Duplicate and shadow tools. A team buys a separate app that overlaps with something Microsoft 365 already includes. Now you pay twice for one job. Unassigned licenses. Seats bought for a hiring plan that slipped, sitting in the tenant with no name attached, quietly renewing. Picture a 40-person firm on Business Standard. Twelve seats belong to people who left or only need email. At the new $14.00 rate, those twelve idle seats cost about $2,016 a year. The 12 percent increase on your whole account adds roughly $720 a year. So the seats you forgot to cancel cost you nearly three times what the price hike does. Cut them, and you more than cover the increase. This is not a problem you solve once. Flexera&#x27;s 2025 State of ITAM report found that 35 percent of organizations say their SaaS waste grew over the past year . The same report found that 56 percent of software asset teams are now rightsizing contracts and subscriptions to claw it back. The drift is constant. So is the fix. Infographic showing wasted SaaS license spend rose 14.2 percent year over year Review before you renew: a right-size playbook You do not need new software to fix this. You need one habit: review the seats before you renew them. Here is the playbook, in order, and most of it takes an afternoon. Pull the usage report. In the Microsoft 365 admin center, run the active-users and license reports. Anyone with no sign-in for 30 to 60 days is a candidate to cut. Reclaim the seats of people who left. Match the license list against your current staff. Remove every name that no longer works there. Match the tier to the work. Move email-only users down to Business Basic. Keep Business Standard or Premium for the people who use the desktop apps or need the security features. Kill the duplicates. List every tool that overlaps with what Microsoft 365 already covers, and cancel the one you are paying for twice. Time it to the renewal. Make the cuts before your term renews after July 1, so you lock the lower count into the new pricing instead of the bloated one. The reason this works is the same reason the waste built up. License sprawl is the top spend challenge IT teams report, and seven in ten of them now treat cutting it as their number-one priority . The price increase just gave you a deadline to do what was already worth doing. The vendor sets the price. You set the headcount and the tier. Right-size before you renew.
