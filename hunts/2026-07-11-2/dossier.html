DPP Data Preflight

“A DPP platform that requires manual data entry across thousands of SKUs will collapse under the weight of a real product catalogue.” — GreenStitch DPP comparison
  • Buyer and pain: Independent fashion brands and their sustainability/production consultants need to collect composition, origin, repairability, and supplier declarations before a passport platform can ingest them.
  • Quantified demand: One implementation guide puts realistic setup at €3,000–€15,000 plus €0.20–€0.50 per piece; another recommends beginning with a 10-SKU pilot. These are vendor/industry estimates, not audited market data.
  • Money row: Brands already pay implementation/setup costs and per-item platform/tag costs; the corpus quotes roughly €0.40 per item at typical volumes in one vendor example.
  • Incumbent weakness: The direct scan found at least six active DPP providers, but their own comparison criteria center ERP/PLM integration and verified upstream data. The wedge is the messy pre-platform intake, not passport hosting.
  • Two-sentence v1: Upload a SKU CSV, invite suppliers to complete missing composition/origin fields, and produce a completeness/error report mapped to a portable DPP-ready schema. Export clean CSV/JSON and an evidence bundle for whichever passport provider the brand later selects.
  • First 100 users: Partner with small fashion sourcing and sustainability consultants; publish “DPP data readiness” checklists and a free 10-SKU preflight.
  • Why now: Textile-specific delegated acts are expected in 2027 followed by a transition period, while brands are being told to build the data foundation now.
  • Confidence: B−. Strong spend and timing, weaker firsthand complaint evidence. Kill fact: three of the leading DPP platforms add equally simple supplier intake and free preflight before customers will pay for a separate layer.

Sources: practical fashion guide, cost/use guide, provider scan.

EUDR Consultant Workspace

“Some SMEs are not required to submit their own DDS. Instead, they must collect and store the DDS reference numbers provided by their upstream suppliers.” — osapiens SME guide
  • Buyer and pain: Independent trade-compliance consultants managing several downstream coffee, cocoa, timber, rubber, or furniture clients need a shared chase-and-evidence workspace without buying an enterprise operator suite for each client.
  • Quantified demand: The corpus says SMEs account for 90% of operators importing regulated products and cites deadlines of December 30, 2026 for some goods and June 30, 2027 for qualifying micro/small firms.
  • Money row: Compared pricing ranges from €29/month and €79/month for a self-serve SME product to approximately €12,000/year for a broader platform.
  • Incumbent weakness: EUDRReady already serves single SME traders and therefore kills the generic concept. The remaining wedge is consultant-first multi-client separation, branded supplier requests, and cross-client deadline/exception views.
  • Two-sentence v1: A consultant creates isolated client workspaces, imports supplier/product lists, sends branded DDS-reference requests, and sees missing/expired evidence across every client. It stores files and reference numbers for five years and exports an audit packet; it does not perform satellite risk analysis or TRACES filing.
  • First 100 users: Reach boutique customs, trade-compliance, and sustainability consultants through EUDR webinars, LinkedIn groups, and downloadable client-readiness packs.
  • Why now: The compliance dates are concrete and the lighter downstream-trader obligation is narrow enough for an upload-and-request web app.
  • Confidence: C+. Excellent timing and a named buyer, but a direct incumbent is close. Kill fact: EUDRReady or another low-cost vendor already offers multi-client consultant tenancy at comparable pricing.

Sources: SME obligations, small-business pricing comparison, direct concept scan.

HoneyBook Exit Mapper

“The Starter plan went from $19/month to $36/month — an 89% increase. The Essentials plan moved to $54/month, and the Premium plan to $81/month.” — Winzi comparison
  • Buyer and pain: Solo photographers, planners, coaches, and creative freelancers want to leave an all-in-one CRM without losing clients, projects, contracts, invoices, and portal history—or discovering after cancellation that exports do not map cleanly.
  • Quantified demand: The pricing change is the strongest switching trigger; the workaround corpus also lists 47 Notion client-portal templates ranging from free to $349.
  • Money row: HoneyBook tiers are quoted at $36/$54/$81 per month; adjacent freelancer suites are quoted at $25–$52 per month, while paid portal workarounds run from single digits to hundreds.
  • Incumbent weakness: The scan found many replacement CRMs, proposal tools, invoicers, and templates, but no verified purpose-built export auditor/migration mapper. Alternatives sell the destination and leave the switching work to the buyer.
  • Two-sentence v1: Upload HoneyBook exports and receive a normalized inventory of clients, projects, files, invoices, contracts, and missing relationships, with destination-specific mapping for a small set of alternatives. Generate a migration checklist, clean import files, a client-link redirect sheet, and a read-only archive before cancellation.
  • First 100 users: HoneyBook-alternative comparison searches, migration checklists in photographer/event-planner communities, and referral partnerships with CRM setup freelancers.
  • Why now: The 2025 price restructuring created switching intent, while a large cheap-template ecosystem shows buyers already assemble partial replacements.
  • Confidence: C+. Sharp job and reachable buyer, but willingness to pay is unverified. Kill fact: HoneyBook exports omit the files or relationship identifiers needed to produce a materially safer migration.

Sources: price-change comparison, workflow comparison, portal workaround market.
