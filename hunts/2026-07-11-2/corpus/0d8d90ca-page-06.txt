Since 1990, an estimated 420 million hectares of forest – an area larger than the EU – have been lost due to conversion to agriculture or other land uses. Although the global rate of deforestation has slowed down over the past 30 years, significant efforts are still required to create a lasting impact. The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) aims to combat deforestation and its far-reaching consequences. To achieve this, companies trading, processing, importing, exporting or distributing products made from the commodities covered by the EUDR or specific derived products need to ensure that their products do not contribute to deforestation or forest degradation.

The commodities regulated under the EUDR include wood, coffee, cocoa, rubber, palm oil, cattle, and soy.

## Step-by-step: How to meet EUDR due diligence requirements

To comply with EUDR regulations, companies need to prove that the materials used in their products do not originate from land that was deforested after December 31, 2020. This involves the following steps:

- Gathering geographical and harvest data as well as data on suppliers, products and orders

- Conducting a risk assessment to evaluate the risk that their products or materials are linked to deforestation or production that doesn’t comply with local laws

- Creating and submitting a Due Diligence Statement (DDS) documenting EUDR compliance and providing the reference numbers to their customers

## Special rules and relief measures for SMEs at a glance

Whether a company is affected by the EUDR does not depend on its size. Therefore, small and medium sized businesses can also be subject to the regulation. In fact, SMEs account for 90% of operators importing EUDR-regulated products. However, certain relief measures may apply to SMEs. Micro and small enterprises that meet at least two of the three criteria below benefit from an extended compliance deadline of June 30, 2027:

- Less than 50 employees

- Annual turnover below €10 million

- Balance sheet total below €10 million

However, if a company’s products were already subject to the EU Timber Regulation (EUTR), the earlier deadline of December 30, 2026 applies — even for smaller businesses.

Some SMEs are not required to submit their own DDS. Instead, they must collect and store the DDS reference numbers provided by their upstream suppliers.
