## What Is the Digital Product Passport and Why Does It Exist?

The Digital Product Passport is a standardised digital record that follows each product throughout its entire lifecycle. According to UNEP, the fashion industry accounts for 10% of global carbon emissions (UNEP, 2023). The DPP exists to force transparency about this impact and accelerate the circular economy.

It forms part of the ESPR (Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation), approved by the EU in 2024, which aims to reduce the environmental impact of industrial products on the European market (European Parliament, 2024). Textiles are one of the priority sectors under this legislation, alongside batteries, electronics and furniture.

In practice, the DPP works as a "digital technical sheet" for the product. It contains structured information about composition, origin, repairability, and end-of-life. Consumers, regulators, and recyclers access that information with a simple QR code scan.

Without the DPP, it's impossible to verify whether a claim like "made with 80% recycled cotton" actually holds up. Traceability stops being optional and becomes verifiable. How many sustainability claims on the market today are impossible to confirm? The Green Claims Directive (also approved 2024) explicitly prohibits unsubstantiated environmental claims from 2026. The DPP is the infrastructure that makes substantiation operational.

## What Should a Textile Product's DPP Contain?

The full requirements are being defined through delegated acts, but the minimum dataset is already known. According to 2024-2025 public consultation documents, fibre composition and country of manufacture are Phase 1 requirements (European Commission, ESPR Delegated Acts Consultation, 2025). Carbon footprint data will be phased in gradually.
